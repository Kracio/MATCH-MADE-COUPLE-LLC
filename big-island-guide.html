Hawai'i Island

A Living Landscape

The Big Island is the only Hawaiian island officially named Hawai'i — and for good reason. It's bigger than all the other islands combined, home to six distinct climate zones, and shaped by six volcanoes: Kilauea, Mauna Loa, Mauna Kea, Hualalai, Kohala, and Kama'ehuakanaloa rising from the sea floor below.

This is the island for travelers who want to stand at the edge of an active crater, swim alongside manta rays at night, drive through coffee farms on volcanic hillsides, and watch the sunset from above the clouds. No two days here look the same — and no other island on the planet offers this kind of raw, living spectacle.

Below, we break it all down: the must-do experiences, the best regions, where to eat, and how to do it all responsibly.

Top Experiences

These are the absolute standouts — the kind of experiences that define a trip to the Big Island.

01

Manta Ray Night Snorkel

One of the most extraordinary wildlife encounters on the planet. Each night, lights attract plankton to the surface near Kona — and the manta rays follow. You float above them as they barrel-roll through the water just inches away. No cages, no captivity — completely wild. Choose an eco-focused operator with small groups and responsible lighting practices.

Manta Ray Night Snorkel 2-Tank Manta Dive (Certified)
02

Hawai'i Volcanoes National Park

Kilauea is the most active volcano on Earth — and you can walk right up to its edge. The park spans Kilauea and Mauna Loa, with trails through lava tubes, past steam vents, and along the Crater Rim. At night, the glow of the caldera turns the sky red. Plan for a full day minimum, bring a jacket — the park sits at 4,000 ft and gets cold and windy.

Live Eruption Cams & Info
03

Mauna Kea Sunrise or Sunset

Nearly 14,000 feet above sea level — and above most of the atmosphere. The cloud inversion below you is unlike anything you'll see from the ground. Dress in proper winter layers (temperatures below freezing, strong winds), acclimatize at the visitor center before continuing, and know that the summit closes 30 minutes after sunset and reopens 30 minutes before sunrise.

04

Kohala Zipline Over Waterfalls

Kohala is the island's oldest and most lush volcano, with emerald valleys carved over a million years. The zipline canopy tours here run above the treetops and — depending on the tour — dip you directly into a private waterfall pool. One of the most visually spectacular adventure experiences on any Hawaiian island.

Zipline + Waterfall Dip Canopy Zipline Tour
05

Real Submarine + Parasailing: Kona

Two of the most unique experiences on the island, both based in Kona. The submarine descends over 100 feet — no wetsuit, no snorkel — for views of reef, turtles, and rays through panoramic windows. Parasailing at 1,200 ft gives you the coastline from above, including the signature "UFO dip" where you skim the surface before soaring back up. Do both in one day.

Kona · After Dark

Manta Rays

Completely wild. Impossibly close. The best wildlife experience in all of Hawai'i — and one of the most remarkable on the planet.

How It Works
The OriginResort lights near Keauhou Bay attracted plankton — and the mantas followed. Today, operators replicate this naturally using underwater lights.
Completely WildNo captivity, no feeding, no touching. The mantas show up on their own to feed — you simply float and watch.
Choose ResponsiblyLook for small-group tours with a genuine conservation focus. Overcrowded sites can stress the mantas and degrade the experience.
The Two Sites
Manta Village — KeauhouThe original site, in front of the Outrigger Kona Resort. Shallower and more consistent for sightings.
Manta Heaven — Makako BayNear Kona Airport (Garden Eel Cove by day). Slightly deeper with larger resident manta population.

Mauna Loa

The world's most active volcano, a living lava tube system, and steam venting from ancient cracks in the Earth. Plan a full day — this place earns it.

Kilauea Must-Dos
Crater Rim TrailThe most dramatic walk in the park — views directly into the Halema'uma'u caldera. Do this first thing in the morning or at dusk.

Hidden Gem · Hualalai

The Volcano Nobody Talks About

Hualalai is the third-youngest volcano on the island — and still very much active. Unlike the other accessible peaks, Hualalai can only be explored by guided tour, which means it stays quiet, pristine, and genuinely off the beaten path. The trail winds through a native cloud forest, past colored lava flows and ancient craters, and into lava tubes carved thousands of years ago. If you want a volcanic experience without the crowds of the National Park, this is it.

Before You Go
Mauna Kea Altitude Real risk of altitude sickness at 13,796 ft. Stop at the Visitor Information Station (9,200 ft) for at least 30 minutes before continuing. Bring serious cold-weather layers.
Mauna Loa: Experienced Only Requires state + NPS permits, high-clearance 4x4, prior high-altitude experience, and full backcountry prep. Not a casual hike — plan accordingly.
Volcanoes NP: Dress for Cold The park sits at 4,000 ft. Wind, rain, and cool temperatures are common year-round. A light jacket minimum, rain layer preferred.
Scuba Divers: 24-Hour Rule If you plan to dive and then ascend to altitude (Mauna Kea or Mauna Loa), wait a full 24 hours after your last dive before ascending above 8,000 ft.
